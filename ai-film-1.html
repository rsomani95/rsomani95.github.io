Introduction


Visual language is the kernel of cinema. Good cinema and effective visual language are mutually inclusive. Cinematography is an intricate art that consists of multiple elements such as shot size, camera angles, camera movement, lighting, character movement, etc. and how all these elements tie together to tell a compelling visual story.

You develop your visual grammar by watching a lot a lot of films, breaking them down to their simplest elements and assessing what makes them effective. This can be an arduous process. This ~5 min sequence alone has about 92 shots!

This is where neural networks offer ample promise. I developed a neural network that focuses on one fundamental element of visual grammar: shot types . It's capable of recognising 6 unique shot types, and is ~91% accurate. The model, validation set, code used to train the network, and some more useful material is available on this GitHub repository.

To the best of my knowledge, there was an attempt at doing this with neural networks, but the dataset was massive (190,000+ images), private, and the output was restricted to only 3 classes. Since the biggest hurdle in tackling this problem was the lack of a dataset, I went through the painstaking process of collecting and manually classifying ~5,700 images.


Neural Networks 101 (for the uninitiated)


"AI" is most often a buzzword for "deep learning", which is the field that deals with using "neural networks" to learn from data. I'll try and summarise the main idea: say you've got some data and want to make sense of it. It could be something structured, like a dataset of customers' purchasing decisions, or something unstructured, like a bunch of images, audio clips medical scans, or video. You may want to predict a customer's desired products, or algorithmically differentiate the image of a dog and a cat, the sound of sea waves and crickets, a video of a goal being scored vs. the goalkeeper saving the day, or whether a tumor is benign or malignant. If this is the case, a neural network is probably the best way to solve the problem.

With a large labelled dataset (1000 images of dogs and cats stored separately), you could train a neural network to learn patterns from these images. The network puts the image through a pile of computation, and spits out two probabilities: P(cat) and P(dog). You calculate how wrong the network was using a loss function, then use calculus (chain rule, or more sophisticatedly backpropagation) to manipulate this pile of computation to produce a more correct output. Do this long enough and with a large enough dataset, and you have in your hands an 'AI' solution to your problem.

If that piqued your interest, I suggest you watch this (~19mins) for a fairly detailed explanation of how a neural network works. If you're bursting with excitement, follow through with this course .

Of course, this isn't all that deep learning is about. What I've described here is supervised learning

There are several other sub-fields of deep learning that are more nuanced and cutting-edge, such as unsupervised learning, reinforcement learning, and generative modelling, to name a few

Methodology


The training process was in sync with fastai's methodology ( transfer learning , data augmentation , fit one cycle policy , learning rate finder , etc). I won't go into too much detail here as this is already very well documented in this Jupyter notebook .

The model used is a pretrained ResNet-50 that was trained using fastai's best practices, most of which are built into the library. The only part of the training process that I haven't seen used extensively, though mentioned by Jeremy in this lecture, was that of progressive image resizing (perhaps cyclical transfer learning is a better term). This is a process where you take a pretrained model (almost always a ResNet-xx pretrained on ImageNet), fine-tune it on your dataset, and then repeat the process with a larger image size. Here's a visual explanation:

You cannot replicate the training process as the training data has not been made public yet. However, you can use my pretrained model to predict shot types on your own images, or to re-evaluate the validation set.


While doing this project, I thought up and implemented a new kind of data augmentation called rgb_randomize that is now part of the fastai library.

This diagram of a ResNet-50 isn't 100% accurate -- it doesn't include skip connections. In a later post, I will explain how a ResNet works with a more complete version of these diagrams.






At each stage, the network is first trained when frozen with a learning rate of ~1e-3 and then unfrozen and fine tuned with a learning rate of ~slice(1e-6, 1e-3)

Heatmaps represent the activations of the neural network. In layman's terms, they show the parts of the image that caused the network to detect its predicted shot type.

Extreme Wide Shot

The network has learnt to recognise patterns on both the ground and the sky.

The foreground is an important factor, as is the human figure when detected.

It's interesting to see the network's behavior in low lighting conditions.

Long Shot

Presence of the entire leg and/or parts of the human body is a determining factor.

The network can detect objects in the bakground.

Predictions remain stable in low-lighting and are resilient to different camera angles and human pose.

Medium Shot

The cleanest predictions are based on the presence of a human face and its spatial relationship with the rest of the frame.

In lower lighting conditions, it uses other parts of the body and/or elements in the background. It isn't restricted to the presence of a face

Medium Close Up

The face is clearly the defining factor. The network focuses in on a more specific part of the face when compared to a Medium Shot.

When the typical human face isn't in the image, it uses other elements in the frame.

Close Up

The face is all the more the defining factor. The network focuses in on an even more specific part of the face when compared to a Medium Close Up.

It uses other elements in the frame when the face is obscure, camera angle isn't the most common one, or in low lighting conditions.

Extreme Close Up

This is harder to interpret due to the diversity of these shots.

The network does a goob job regardless, it scored 95/100 with ECUs.



Robustness

It's important to take these results with a pinch of salt. The validation set, while diverse, is not comprehensive. For all shots besides an Extreme Wide Shot or an Extreme Close Up, most of the training samples were images that had humans in them. Thought this is usually the case in movies, it isn't so all the time.

I've also refrained from including non standard compositions from the training and validation set for two reasons: they represent a minority of shots, and they are difficult to classify. That being said, the model performs well on most commonly found shot types across most cinema.

The model could be biased against "foreign" (non-Hollywood) cinema, as it was trained on mostly Hollywood movie stills. However, the heatmaps above show that it is able to detect fundamental features that are seen across all cinema. I haven't tested it thoroughly enough to make a definitive statement regarding this.


The Bigger Picture


My goal is to create tools that help deconstruct movies in a way that isn't just another interesting way of looking at it for film enthusiasts, but that could help filmmakers hone their craft, better understand why cinema works, and to ultimately create better films.

A shot type classifier is just one tool. When sticking to single frame analysis, one could build classifiers for recognising camera focus , OTS (over-the-shoulder) shots, camera angles , etc.

Moving over to the video domain (which is nothing but a series of still images), it is feasible to detect camera movements -- zoom, pan, tilt, tracking, swish- pan, etc., and camera mechanisms -- slider, drone, handheld, gimbal, crane, etc. There is a rich literature on shot boundary (cut) detection, but it hasn't yet been adopted for cinema. Average shot lengths have been worked on quite a bit, as seen here , but this was done manually, and can only be so reliable. For filmmakers, cut detection in combination with shot recognition and the aforementioned applications could help put together a powerful dataset such as this:

A dataset with even some of these metrics, when created on a large scale and grouped/split sensibly, could help build a detailed visual profile of any filmmaker. Perhaps you could build a recommender system that gives you a boiler plate shot breakdown based on the kind of scene you want to shoot.

Barry Salt's Database is an aggregation of shot types and camera movements, but it is an aggregation, not a time-series, which makes it useful in an academic context, but not very conducive to practical application.

Fredric Brodbeck's Cinemetrics project is a series of stunning visualisations using film as a data source (and a major source of inspitation for this project). It would be interesting to convert his ideas to another tool in the filmmaker's toolkit.

Conclusion


In this post, I took you through a neural network that can recognise shot types to a respectable degree. In the last section, I tried to show the immense potential that modern computational techniques hold for better understanding/ creation of cinema. What's most exciting about this is that all of this is software-driven, thus easily accessible.

I'm releasing the code and the model here . If you know some basic Python, you should be able to use it to detect shot types of any image you like. Work is underway to increase the kinds of shots it can detect, and to release a web application where you can do the same with no knowledge of programming.
